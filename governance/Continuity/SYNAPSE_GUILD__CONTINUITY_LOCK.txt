================================================================================
0) PURPOSE OF THIS DOCUMENT
================================================================================

This document defines what a CONTINUITY LOCK is, why it exists, and how it is used.

After reading this document, the phrase “Create a Continuity Lock” must be sufficient
instruction for any compliant operator (human or AI).

A Continuity Lock exists to solve one problem:

> How do we continue a complex conversation and its consequences when the chat itself
> cannot be carried forward?

Chats end. Memory is unreliable. Context decays. Decisions get re-argued.
Continuity Locks prevent that.

This is governance law.

================================================================================
1) CORE DEFINITION (CANON)
================================================================================

1.1 Continuity Lock — definition
A Continuity Lock is a state-sealing governance artifact that preserves continuity
across chats by explicitly declaring what is true, binding, relevant, and next,
without relying on chat history.

A Continuity Lock allows a new session to continue an old session’s direction
as if the history were present, even when it is not.

One-sentence definition:
A Continuity Lock preserves project continuity without chat history by sealing
state, decisions, scope, and the resume point.

================================================================================
2) WHAT PROBLEM IT SOLVES (WHY IT EXISTS)
================================================================================

Continuity Locks exist because:

- AI chat sessions have finite length.
- Chat history is not guaranteed to persist or be readable.
- Humans and AI forget *why* decisions were made.
- Re-explaining context causes drift, re-litigation, and accidental redesign.

Without a Continuity Lock, new sessions tend to:
- revisit rejected ideas
- reinterpret settled decisions
- wander into new scope unintentionally
- lose momentum and mode

A Continuity Lock prevents all of this by acting as a state seal.

================================================================================
3) WHAT A CONTINUITY LOCK IS — AND IS NOT
================================================================================

3.1 What it IS
A Continuity Lock is:
- a state declaration
- a boundary marker
- a resume instruction
- a compression of meaning, not of conversation

3.2 What it is NOT
A Continuity Lock is NOT:
- a transcript
- a summary of everything discussed
- a brainstorming log
- a task list
- an execution plan
- a Bootstrap Prompt

It does not preserve *conversation*.
It preserves *consequence*.

================================================================================
4) WHAT A CONTINUITY LOCK FREEZES
================================================================================

A valid Continuity Lock must explicitly state:

4.1 Current state
- Where we are right now
- What phase or activity we are in (e.g., Control Sync complete; execution pending)

4.2 Binding decisions
- Decisions that are now law
- Constraints that must not be revisited casually

4.3 Non-binding or deferred items
- Ideas explored but rejected
- Ideas deferred intentionally
- Topics that are no longer relevant

4.4 Active vs inactive work
- What is in progress
- What has not started
- What is complete

4.5 Required reading for the next session
- Which snapshots, packs, or documents must be read first

4.6 Resume point
- The exact next action or question the next session should address

If any of the above is missing, continuity will degrade.

================================================================================
5) IMPLICIT HISTORY VS EXPLICIT STATE
================================================================================

A key principle:

A Continuity Lock does not carry history forward.
It carries the *implications* of that history forward.

Learning, discussion, and exploration during a chat are allowed to decay
unless they are promoted into one of the following:
- a binding decision
- a constraint
- a note about rejection or deferral
- a next-step instruction

Rule:
If it matters later, it must be written into the Continuity Lock or a Snapshot.

================================================================================
6) RELATIONSHIP TO SNAPSHOTS
================================================================================

Continuity Locks and Snapshots are complementary but distinct.

Snapshots:
- record what happened
- preserve rationale
- provide auditability

Continuity Locks:
- declare what is now binding
- declare what no longer matters
- tell the next session how to resume

Typical pattern:
- Control Sync Snapshot records decisions and reasoning
- Continuity Lock distills those into a state seal for the next session

A Continuity Lock may reference Snapshots.
It does not replace them.

================================================================================
7) RELATIONSHIP TO EXECUTION PACKS
================================================================================

Execution Packs and Continuity Locks solve different problems.

Execution Packs:
- constrain how work is executed
- prevent implementation drift
- may span multiple files and steps

Continuity Locks:
- constrain interpretation
- prevent decision drift
- define what execution context applies

A Continuity Lock may state:
- “An Execution Pack exists and is binding”
- “Execution Pack X must be read before proceeding”

But the Lock itself does not contain execution instructions.

================================================================================
8) RELATIONSHIP TO BOOTSTRAP PROMPTS
================================================================================

Continuity Locks and Bootstrap Prompts are often used together, but they are not the same.

Continuity Lock answers:
- “What is true?”
- “What is binding?”
- “Where are we resuming?”

Bootstrap Prompt answers:
- “How should the AI behave now?”
- “What role is it in?”
- “What should it do first?”

A Continuity Lock is content and state.
A Bootstrap Prompt is behavior and stance.

================================================================================
9) WHEN TO CREATE A CONTINUITY LOCK
================================================================================

You SHOULD create a Continuity Lock when:
- a chat is nearing its limit
- a Control Sync has concluded
- work will resume later
- execution depends on preserved context
- a mode or direction must not change

You MAY skip a Continuity Lock only when:
- the work is trivial
- nothing binding was decided
- no continuity is required

================================================================================
10) HOW A CONTINUITY LOCK IS USED IN A NEW SESSION
================================================================================

Typical rehydration flow:

1) Start a new chat/session
2) Upload supporting artifacts as needed (snapshots, packs, repos)
3) Provide the Continuity Lock
4) Provide a Bootstrap Prompt (if behavior must be initialized)
5) Proceed from the resume point declared in the Lock

Rule:
The Continuity Lock is read first.
It defines reality.

================================================================================
11) FORMAT AND STORAGE (RECOMMENDED, NOT RIGID)
================================================================================

A Continuity Lock is typically a single text document.

Recommended naming:
CONTINUITY_LOCK__<SCOPE>__<TOPIC>__YYYY-MM-DD.txt

Recommended storage:
- `<Subject>_Data/Latest Rehydration Pack/` (active / current)
- `<Subject>_Data/Archive/` (archived, optional)

Rule: The Governance Pack stays stateless.
Do not store subject-specific locks inside the Governance Pack.

When a new Continuity Lock supersedes an old one:
- archive the old lock
- activate the new lock

================================================================================
12) QUALITY BAR (WHAT MAKES A GOOD LOCK)
================================================================================

A good Continuity Lock:
- makes the next session boring
- prevents re-argument
- prevents scope creep
- allows seamless continuation without anxiety

A bad Continuity Lock:
- is vague
- reads like a summary
- does not state what is irrelevant
- does not specify next action

================================================================================
13) COMMON FAILURE MODES (AND HOW TO AVOID THEM)
================================================================================

Failure: Treating the lock like a summary
Fix: Declare state and decisions, not discussion.

Failure: Carrying everything forward “just in case”
Fix: Explicitly mark what is deferred or irrelevant.

Failure: No clear resume point
Fix: End the lock with “Next step: …”

================================================================================
14) CANONICAL COMMANDS
================================================================================

Once this law is accepted, the following phrases are valid:

- “Create a Continuity Lock.”
Meaning:
Seal the current state so the next session can resume without chat history.

- “Update the Continuity Lock.”
Meaning:
State has changed; create a new lock and archive the old one.

- “Rehydrate from this Continuity Lock.”
Meaning:
Treat it as authoritative state and proceed accordingly.

================================================================================
0) PURPOSE OF THIS DOCUMENT
================================================================================

This document makes the phrase “Execution Pack” carry a fully-loaded meaning.

Once you have read and accepted this law, you do not need to re-explain Execution Packs.
You can say:
  “We need an Execution Pack for this Control Sync.”
…and any compliant operator (human or AI) will know what to do.

Execution Packs exist to solve a brutal reality:

- Control Syncs can be long.
- Memory decays (human and AI).
- Chats end.
- Context drifts.
- Implementation improvisation produces hacks, god-files, and architectural backsliding.

Execution Packs convert a long, nuanced Control Sync into a deterministic, resumable,
anti-drift execution state.

This is governance law. It applies across all projects (<SUBJECT>, <SUBJECT>, games, anything).

================================================================================
0.1) ARTIFACT LAW (BINDING)
================================================================================

Execution Packs are subject-state artifacts.

- If the Execution Pack does not exist as file(s) on disk, the pack does not exist.
- Chat text describing a pack is PROPOSAL only until written to canonical storage.
- If a pack is referenced but cannot be proven present + readable, trigger Disclosure Gate
  and treat the pack as unavailable.

================================================================================
1) DEFINITIONS (CANON)
================================================================================

1.1 Execution Pack (definition)
An Execution Pack is a time-bound, consumable bundle of implementation constraints
and execution instructions that converts Control Sync decisions into a deterministic
execution path across time, chats, agents, and humans.

Execution Packs are authored, not filled out.

They are not rigidly templated, because their contents must match the specific
situation and risk profile of the work they govern.

1.2 Control Sync (reference)
A Control Sync is where decisions are made deliberately. Execution Packs typically
originate from a Control Sync (directly or immediately after it).

1.3 Guild Orders / Raid / Dungeon / Quest (reference)
Guild Orders describe objectives for a phase/version.
A Raid is executing Guild Orders.
Dungeons are major work slices inside Guild Orders.
Quests are atomic executable commitments.

Execution Packs are not Guild Orders and not Quests.
Execution Packs are the “anti-drift work order” that can constrain how a Raid/Quest
is executed when drift risk is high.

1.4 Snapshots (reference)
Snapshots are the audit trail of what happened:
- Control Sync Snapshots: decisions and rationale
- EOD Snapshots: what was built/tested and what is now true
- General Snapshots: governance changes

Execution Packs do not replace Snapshots.
Execution Packs constrain execution; Snapshots record truth after execution.

1.5 Continuity Lock / Bootstrap Prompt (reference)
- Continuity Lock: state seal (“what is binding and where we resume”)
- Bootstrap Prompt: stance initializer (“how the next session must behave”)

Execution Packs are commonly referenced by Continuity Locks and enforced by Bootstrap
Prompts, but they are distinct artifacts.

================================================================================
2) THE CORE INTENT (WHAT AN EXECUTION PACK DOES)
================================================================================

Execution Packs exist to do three things:

A) Compress context into executable law
A Control Sync can produce dozens of decisions, constraints, and “don’t do that” rules.
The Pack compresses those into a form that can be executed later without re-arguing.

B) Remove discretion where discretion causes drift
Execution Packs intentionally reduce improvisation:
- the order of work is constrained
- what cannot be touched is explicit
- what counts as “done” is explicit
- what evidence/tests are required is explicit

C) Preserve the “why” enough to prevent backsliding
Execution Packs are not philosophy documents, but they must preserve the minimal
rationale required to prevent a future operator from “cleaning up” into disaster.

One sentence summary:
Execution Packs convert decisions into a resumable, deterministic execution state.

================================================================================
3) WHEN AN EXECUTION PACK IS WARRANTED (TRIGGERS)
================================================================================

Execution Packs are optional; they are not required for every piece of work.

An Execution Pack is warranted when one or more of the following is true:

3.1 Drift risk is high
- The work spans multiple days or chats.
- The system is complex enough that “we’ll remember” is a lie.
- There are many non-obvious constraints.

3.2 The work is substrate/platform-sensitive
- Engine primitives are missing.
- The temptation to hack a module into the engine is high.
- The architecture has strong invariants (no god files, strict separation of WHAT/HOW).

3.3 Verification/auditability matters
- You must prove that claims are grounded (truth gating, evidence/citations).
- You need deterministic artifacts, schemas, and immutability rules.

3.4 The scope is multi-step with dependencies
- There are staged deliverables (“Batch 0A, 0B, 0C…” style).
- Later steps depend on earlier scaffolding being correct.

3.5 You are handing work across operators
- Different humans (teammates) will execute.
- Different AI sessions or different models will execute.
- You will resume after a long gap.

3.6 “We must not refactor” or “we must not redesign” is a hard constraint
When you need to freeze behavior and forbid creative rewrites, use an Execution Pack.

An Execution Pack is usually unnecessary when:
- the work is trivial and can be done in one sitting with low risk
- the work is exploratory brainstorming only (no execution planned)
- the work is already fully locked and simple enough to follow with existing canon

================================================================================
4) WHAT AN EXECUTION PACK IS NOT
================================================================================

Execution Packs are not:
- a Codex
- a Canonical Vision
- a marketing doc
- a roadmap
- a backlog
- a quest template
- a summary dump of everything discussed

Execution Packs are not permanent law.
They are operational law for a specific execution window.

Execution Packs are also not “one magic file.”
A Pack may be one file or many. “Pack” means bundle.

================================================================================
5) SHAPE AND ALLOWED FORMS (NO TEMPLATE LAW)
================================================================================

Execution Packs are intentionally non-templated.
Their contents must be dictated by the work.

However, a Pack must still be recognizable and complete.

An Execution Pack may include any combination of the following, as needed:

5.1 Runbook / batch plan
- ordered steps (“Batch A baseline”, “Batch B storage adapter”, etc.)
- commands to run
- expected outputs (“paste back” requirements)
- acceptance criteria per batch

5.2 Contracts / invariants
- schemas and required fields
- immutability rules
- evidence/citation requirements
- naming conventions and ID rules
- adapter/profile constraints
- “no silent overwrite” storage rules

5.3 Guardrails / non-goals / tripwires
- explicit “do not touch X”
- explicit “do not refactor Y”
- explicit “do not redesign architecture”
- explicit “do not introduce cloud egress without consent”

5.4 Verification requirements
- smoke tests and exact test files to run
- manual verification steps
- “definition of done” checklist

5.5 Handoff + rehydrate protocol
- required uploads (zips/docs)
- what to read first
- how to orient to repo tree
- what to do if files differ from expected (fail fast)

5.6 Implementation map
- bridge document between canon and repo reality
- “target tree” vs “current tree” and transition strategy

5.7 Risk log (minimal)
- known failure modes
- what to do if blocked
- “do not workaround by violating invariants”

5.8 Pack Index / Read Order (REQUIRED when multi-file)
If an Execution Pack contains more than one file, it MUST include a single top-level
index file (name is flexible) that a new operator can open first.

This index file must include:
- purpose of the pack (one paragraph)
- the ordered reading list (“read these in this order”)
- a contents list (all pack files/folders)
- what the operator should do first after reading (next action)

Rationale:
A “pack” is only useful if it is navigable. Do not hand someone an unstructured pile
of documents and call it done.

A Pack may be one file (single-file pack). In that case, the “index” is simply the top
sections of the file.

A Pack must be coherent and bounded.
If it becomes a novel, it is drifting into Codex territory.

================================================================================
6) MINIMUM VIABLE EXECUTION PACK (MVEP)
================================================================================

Even though packs are not templated, every Execution Pack must answer these questions.

If a Pack does not answer these, it is incomplete.

MVEP checklist:

6.1 Identity
- What is this pack called?
- What scope does it govern? (System: <SUBJECT> / Module: <SUBJECT> / Other)
- What triggered it? (one paragraph)

6.2 Objective
- What must be true when this pack is “complete”?
- What is explicitly out of scope?

6.3 Inputs / prerequisites
- What artifacts/repos/zips must be loaded?
- What must be read first?

6.4 Execution boundaries
- What must NOT be changed?
- What refactors are forbidden?
- What rules are non-negotiable?

6.5 Execution plan
- What is the ordered sequence of steps/batches?
- What does each step produce?

6.6 Verification
- What tests/proofs are required?
- What output is expected from verification?

6.7 Stop/Archive condition
- When is this pack obsolete?
- Where does it get archived?
- What artifact(s) become the new truth after completion (snapshots, updated repo zip, etc.)?

6.8 Navigability
- If the pack is multi-file, does it include a Pack Index / Read Order file?

If all eight are satisfied, the pack is valid even if it looks “different” than others.

================================================================================
7) LIFECYCLE LAW (ACTIVE → OBSOLETE → ARCHIVED)
================================================================================

7.1 Creation
Execution Packs are created during or immediately after a Control Sync when the
need for deterministic execution is identified.

7.2 Active use
While active, the Execution Pack is binding for the execution it governs.
Operators (human or AI) must not improvise beyond allowed discretion.

7.2.1 Activation must be explicit (anti-guess)
An Execution Pack is ACTIVE only when the current execution state explicitly names
it as binding for this execution window.

Valid sources include (any one is sufficient):
- a Control Sync decision captured in the Control Sync Snapshot
- the Bootstrap Prompt
- the Continuity Lock

If no canonical artifact names an active pack, you MUST NOT assume one is active.

7.3 Completion
A pack is complete when its objective/deliverables are met and verification passes.

7.4 Archive
Execution Packs are consumable. They must be archived after completion.

Reason:
A completed pack is no longer an instruction; it is historical record.
Reusing old packs blindly causes wrong constraints to be applied to new work.

Archival is not deletion.
Archived packs are retained for audit and to reuse patterns (not rules).

7.5 Update
If a pack must change mid-execution:
- do a Control Sync (or mini Control Sync) and record the change
- update the pack contents (in place; do not create parallel packs)
- ensure the Continuity Lock references the updated state

7.6 “Packs do not create new system law” rule (IMPORTANT)
An Execution Pack may constrain execution, but it must not silently create or modify
system law (Codex, Canonical Vision, or governance canon).

If execution reveals a new rule/invariant that must become permanent:
- pause execution (or isolate the finding)
- run a Control Sync
- update the appropriate canonical document(s)
- then resume with an updated pack (if needed)

================================================================================
8) RELATIONSHIP TO OTHER ARTIFACTS (HOW IT FITS IN THE SYSTEM)
================================================================================

Execution Pack is part of the Synapse Guild Operating System (process), not the Guild body.

Canonical relationships:

- Control Sync produces decisions.
- Execution Pack converts those decisions into executable constraints + run plans.
- Guild Orders define the broader campaign objectives (phase/version).
- Quests define atomic commitments.
- Daily execution (Dailies loop) executes chosen scope.
- Snapshots record what happened and what is now true.
- Continuity Lock seals state for cross-chat resumption.
- Bootstrap Prompt initializes the next session’s stance to obey the above.

Execution Pack does NOT replace Guild Orders/Quests/Snapshots.
It constrains execution so those artifacts can be produced without drift.

================================================================================
9) REHYDRATION ROLE (HOW EXECUTION PACKS ARE USED ACROSS CHATS)
================================================================================

Execution Packs are often included inside a Rehydration Pack.

A Rehydration Pack is a bundle (not a separate required file) that contains the
minimum artifacts to resume work without drift.

Execution Pack’s role inside rehydration:
- it tells the new operator “how to execute” the bounded work
- it prevents re-deciding or improvising
- it ensures verification and boundaries remain enforced

Typical rehydration bundle for execution:
- Continuity Lock (state + resume point)
- Bootstrap Prompt (stance + read order)
- Active Execution Pack (if active work requires it)
- Latest Snapshots (Control Sync + EOD relevant)
- Active Guild Orders (if raid active)
- Accepted Quests (commitments)
- Relevant Codex/Vision/contracts
- Updated repo/data zip(s)

================================================================================
10) DISCRETION LAW (WHAT THE OPERATOR MAY DECIDE)
================================================================================

Execution Packs remove discretion in drift-sensitive areas, but they do not eliminate
all discretion. A pack must define where the operator can decide.

Allowed discretion (typical):
- minor implementation details that do not violate invariants
- formatting and ergonomics that do not change behavior
- test organization improvements that do not alter semantics
- batching output to avoid truncation

Forbidden discretion (typical):
- refactoring architecture when the pack says “no refactor”
- changing immutability/truth rules to “make it easier”
- bypassing consent rules for network egress
- claiming work was done without verification artifacts/results
- silently overwriting durable artifacts
- changing module boundaries (WHAT/HOW separation) without governance decision

If an operator hits a decision that feels “architectural,” pause and trigger a Control Sync.

================================================================================
11) PACK STRUCTURE AND STORAGE (FILE SYSTEM LAW)
================================================================================

Execution Packs are subject-state artifacts.
They belong in <Subject>_Data (not in <Subject>_Engine and not in Governance).

Execution Packs should have first-class storage, like Snapshots and Guild Orders.

Canonical default storage (inside the SUBJECT data bundle):
- `<Subject>_Data/Docs/Execution Packs/Active/`
- `<Subject>_Data/Docs/Execution Packs/Archived/`

Optional convenience mirror:
- The single most recent pack (or its pointer file) may be placed in `<Subject>_Data/Latest Rehydration Pack/`.

A Pack is usually a folder (or a set of files) with a shared prefix.

Naming convention (recommended):
EXEC_PACK__<SCOPE>__<TOPIC>__YYYY-MM-DD__vX/
or for single-file packs:
EXEC_PACK__<SCOPE>__<TOPIC>__YYYY-MM-DD__vX.txt

Examples:
- EXEC_PACK__<SUBJECT>__BATCH_0_ENGINE_EXPANSION__2026-01-13__v1/
- EXEC_PACK__<SUBJECT>__V1_CONTRACTS_AND_PIPELINE__2026-01-13__v1/

Archive convention:
When complete, move the entire pack folder into Archived unchanged.

Rule:
Active packs must be obvious to find.
Archived packs must be preserved, not edited.

12) CASE STUDIES (WHY PACKS DIFFER)
================================================================================

These are examples to demonstrate that packs are situational by design.

12.1 <SUBJECT> “Batch 0 Engine Expansion” pack (platform substrate)
Trigger:
<SUBJECT> could not be built cleanly because <SUBJECT> lacked platform primitives.
Constraint:
No <SUBJECT>-specific code in core. Additive changes only. No router refactor.
Contents:
StorageAdapter, Results, Profiles/Consent, Truth spine, Module registry, Meetings session stub.
Outcome:
Pack archived after primitives implemented and smoke tests passed.

12.2 <SUBJECT> v1 contracts pack (module build invariants)
Trigger:
Long design conversation with high drift risk and many subtle invariants.
Constraint:
Artifact immutability, evidence/citations, indexing minimum, resume rules, adapter matrix.
Contents:
Artifact versioning, error taxonomy/resume, ID conventions, retrieval/indexing contract,
truth/evidence contract, profile/adapter matrix, architecture mapping.
Outcome:
Pack remains active until <SUBJECT> v1 raid is completed; then archived.

12.3 Hypothetical game build pack (design-to-implementation freeze)
Trigger:
Long Control Sync defining world rules, progression, and core constraints.
Constraint:
Forbid premature coding of features until rules are locked; preserve design invariants.
Contents:
Rules of the world, entity constraints, state/save model, staging plan (content first, code later),
verification steps (golden tests or sim harness).
Outcome:
Pack archived when the phase is complete; a new pack may be authored for the next phase.

The difference is the point: packs solve drift for a specific execution window.

================================================================================
13) OPERATOR COMMANDS (CANONICAL PHRASES)
================================================================================

Once this law is accepted, the following phrases are valid commands:

- “Generate an Execution Pack for this Control Sync.”
Meaning:
Create the pack’s file(s), name them, and ensure the pack is navigable and complete.
If the pack is multi-file, produce a Pack Index / Read Order file first.
Ensure the pack satisfies MVEP (Section 6) before declaring it “ready.”

- “We need an Execution Pack for this Control Sync.”
Meaning:
Same as above. It is valid shorthand once the term is understood.

- “Activate this Execution Pack.”
Meaning:
Treat it as binding for the current work; obey constraints; produce required proofs.

- “Archive this Execution Pack.”
Meaning:
The pack’s objective is done; move it to Archived; record EOD snapshot; continue.

- “Update the Execution Pack.”
Meaning:
A Control Sync decision changed constraints; update the pack; re-lock continuity.

================================================================================
14) QUALITY BAR (WHAT MAKES A PACK “GOOD”)
================================================================================

A good Execution Pack makes execution boring.

You should be able to:
- resume after a week and not re-decide
- hand the pack to another operator and get consistent output
- prove what happened via tests/artifacts/snapshots
- avoid “quick hacks” because boundaries are explicit

A bad Execution Pack:
- is a vague summary without constraints
- does not specify what is forbidden
- does not specify verification
- forces re-interpretation during execution
- causes “we forgot why we did this” backsliding

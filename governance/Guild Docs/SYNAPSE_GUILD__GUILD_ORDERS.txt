================================================================================
0) PURPOSE OF THIS DOCUMENT
================================================================================

This document defines what GUILD ORDERS are, why they exist, and how they are used.

After reading this document, the phrase “Create Guild Orders” must be sufficient
instruction for any compliant operator (human or AI).

Guild Orders are a core governance primitive of the Synapse Guild Operating System.
They define WHAT we are committing to build, not HOW we will build it.

This is governance law.

================================================================================
1) CORE DEFINITION (CANON)
================================================================================

1.1 Guild Orders — definition
Guild Orders are a scope-defining governance artifact that declares the intended
objectives, constraints, and definition of success for a body of work.

Guild Orders answer one question:

> “What are we building in this phase, and how will we know it is done?”

One-sentence definition:
Guild Orders define the authoritative scope and intent of a build effort.

================================================================================
2) WHAT PROBLEM GUILD ORDERS SOLVE
================================================================================

Guild Orders exist because:

- Complex systems cannot be built safely without a shared definition of scope.
- Without a declared scope, work drifts into improvisation and contradiction.
- Teams (human or AI) need a stable reference for what “success” means.
- Decisions made in conversation must be elevated into durable intent.

Without Guild Orders:
- work becomes reactive
- priorities shift silently
- “nice ideas” sneak into critical paths
- completion is ambiguous

Guild Orders prevent scope drift.

================================================================================
3) WHAT GUILD ORDERS ARE — AND ARE NOT
================================================================================

3.1 What Guild Orders ARE
Guild Orders are:
- an intent declaration
- a scope boundary
- a success definition
- a coordination artifact

3.2 What Guild Orders are NOT
Guild Orders are NOT:
- a task list
- a step-by-step plan
- a process description
- a quest backlog
- an execution script
- an Execution Pack

Guild Orders do not prescribe HOW work is done.
They declare WHAT must be accomplished.

================================================================================
4) STRUCTURE: RAIDS AND DUNGEONS (CANON)
================================================================================

4.1 Raids
A RAID is the execution state of a Guild Orders document.

Raid states:
- ACTIVE — Guild Orders are currently being executed
- PAUSED — execution is intentionally halted
- COMPLETED — all objectives are satisfied

A Raid is not a section inside the document.
A Raid is the act of executing the Guild Orders.

4.2 Dungeons
Within Guild Orders, scope is divided into DUNGEONS.

A Dungeon is:
- a major slice of scope
- a self-contained objective area
- independently executable if dependencies allow

Dungeons:
- do NOT imply sequence
- do NOT imply priority unless explicitly stated
- may be raided in any dependency-safe order

This replaces “phases,” “steps,” or numbered GO sections.

================================================================================
5) VARIABILITY AND NON-TEMPLATED NATURE
================================================================================

Guild Orders are intentionally non-templated.

Reason:
The shape of Guild Orders must match the nature of the work.

Examples:
- A structural refactor may have Dungeons focused on filesystem hygiene.
- A product build may have Dungeons focused on features or subsystems.
- A research-heavy effort may have exploratory Dungeons alongside build Dungeons.

Uniformity is not a goal.
Clarity is the goal.

A Guild Orders document may include:
- definitions of done
- build laws / constraints
- non-goals
- risk notes
- dependency notes

But it must not become a procedural checklist.

================================================================================
6) RELATIONSHIP TO OTHER ARTIFACTS
================================================================================

6.1 Where Guild Orders live (state by location)
Guild Orders live in Subject Data under:
- <Subject>_Data/Guild Orders/ACTIVE/
- <Subject>_Data/Guild Orders/PAUSED/
- <Subject>_Data/Guild Orders/COMPLETED/

State is defined by location:
- If a Raid file is in ACTIVE, it is the current live plan being executed.
- If a Raid file is in PAUSED, it is intentionally stopped and must not be executed until resumed.
- If a Raid file is in COMPLETED, it is done (all dungeons + quests complete + verified).

Multi-raid rule:
- Multiple raids may exist at once.
- Multiple raids may be ACTIVE at once, but scope for a given day/session is chosen in Control Sync.

6.2 Completion rule (ACTIVE/PAUSED → COMPLETED)
A Raid is complete when its dungeons and quests are complete AND verified.
When a Raid completes:
- move the Raid file into <Subject>_Data/Guild Orders/COMPLETED/
- record the move in the End-of-Day Snapshot.

Guild Orders sit between vision and execution.

Canonical relationships:

- Codex / Canonical Vision:
  Define what the system is and why it exists.

- Guild Orders:
  Define what is being built now and what “done” means.

- Execution Packs:
  Constrain how Guild Orders are executed when drift risk is high.

- Quests:
  Atomic commitments created under Guild Orders.

- Dailies Loop:
  Day-to-day execution rhythm.

- Snapshots:
  Record what happened while executing Guild Orders.

Guild Orders define scope.
They do not replace or override these other artifacts.

================================================================================
7) WHEN TO CREATE GUILD ORDERS
================================================================================

You SHOULD create Guild Orders when:
- beginning a major build effort
- starting a new version or milestone
- committing to a non-trivial objective
- coordinating work across days, chats, or agents

You SHOULD NOT create Guild Orders for:
- trivial fixes
- one-off experiments
- isolated tasks that do not affect broader scope

Guild Orders are for commitment, not brainstorming.

================================================================================
8) HOW GUILD ORDERS ARE USED IN PRACTICE
================================================================================

Typical lifecycle:

1) Control Sync
- Decisions are made.
- Scope is agreed upon.

2) Guild Orders are authored
- Objectives and Dungeons are written.
- Definition of done is clarified.

3) A Raid begins
- Guild Orders move to ACTIVE.
- Dungeons are selected for execution.

4) Quests are created
- Atomic work is derived from Dungeons.
- Quests are Accepted intentionally.

5) Execution proceeds via Dailies
- Scope → implement → test → snapshot → stop.

6) Completion
- All Dungeons are satisfied.
- Raid is marked COMPLETED.
- Final snapshot records outcomes.

================================================================================
9) ADDING OR MODIFYING SCOPE
================================================================================

Guild Orders may evolve, but changes are governed.

Rules:
- Scope changes require a Control Sync.
- New Dungeons may be added if intent expands.
- Existing Dungeons may be clarified or split.
- Silent scope changes are forbidden.

Guild Orders are living documents, but not casual ones.

================================================================================
10) QUALITY BAR (WHAT MAKES GOOD GUILD ORDERS)
================================================================================

Good Guild Orders:
- make scope explicit
- make success measurable
- prevent random work
- give context to every Quest

Bad Guild Orders:
- read like a to-do list
- lack a definition of done
- mix execution steps into intent
- allow “drive-by” additions without review

================================================================================
11) CANONICAL COMMANDS
================================================================================

Once this law is accepted, the following phrases are valid:

- “Create Guild Orders.”
Meaning:
Define the authoritative scope and objectives for a build effort.

- “Start a Raid.”
Meaning:
Begin executing the Guild Orders.

- “Pause the Raid.”
Meaning:
Intentionally halt execution without discarding intent.

- “Complete the Raid.”
Meaning:
All Dungeons are satisfied; record final snapshot.
